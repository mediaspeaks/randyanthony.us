Reliability Is Not Improvised

Reliable organizations rarely become reliable by accident. They are intentionally designed, carefully observed, consistently maintained, and continuously improved.

Drawing on decades inside broadcast television, master control, automation, engineering support, and real-time operational environments, this book introduces a practical framework for understanding operational reliability from the inside out.

Rather than focusing on abstract management theory, the book examines operational visibility, human performance, decision making, dependencies, operational drift, recovery, resilience, and the everyday disciplines that keep complex organizations functioning under pressure.

Whether you work in broadcasting, manufacturing, healthcare, technology, transportation, or any organization that depends upon people, processes, and technology working together, the principles remain remarkably consistent.

FM: The Fading Modulation of Terrestrial Radio

For nearly a century, terrestrial radio informed, entertained, connected communities, and introduced generations of listeners to new music, new ideas, and trusted local personalities. It became one of America's most influential communication systems.

The Telecommunications Act of 1996 fundamentally reshaped that system. Consolidation, automation, economic pressures, new technologies, and changing audience behavior transformed an industry that had long depended on local ownership, live personalities, and community engagement.

This book examines those changes through the lens of operational systems rather than nostalgia. Instead of asking only what changed, it explores why those changes occurred and how operational decisions compounded over time.

The lessons extend well beyond broadcasting. Every complex organization eventually faces pressure to centralize, automate, reduce costs, increase efficiency, and operate with fewer people. Radio's evolution offers valuable insight into those broader operational challenges.
